Business Acquisition

Capital purpose-built for purchasing an existing enterprise. Finance up to 90% of the acquisition price. Terms structured to let the acquired business service its own facility.

Apply Now →

Buy an operating business. Skip the startup risk.

Acquiring an existing enterprise is the most capital-efficient path to scale — you inherit an established customer base, documented revenue streams, trained personnel, and operational infrastructure from day one. Acquisition financing makes this possible without liquidating personal assets or diluting equity.

We work with institutions that specialize in transaction financing, including SBA 7(a) lenders whose programs are explicitly designed for business purchases. The terms are structured so the acquired entity's own cash flow services the debt — the business pays for itself.

  • ✓ Maturities extending to 10–25 years through SBA programs
  • ✓ Working capital often included in the facility
  • ✓ The target company's cash flow services the obligation

Financing structures for acquisition.

The right structure depends on the target's size, industry, and your capital position.

🏛️

SBA 7(a) Acquisition

The most common path. Government-backed with a down payment as low as 10%. Rates and terms among the most favorable in commercial lending.

  • Up to $5M facility size
  • 10–25 year maturity
  • Down payment from 10%
📋

Conventional Acquisition

Institutional financing outside SBA parameters. Faster to close, fewer documentation requirements, suitable for larger transactions.

  • No SBA program caps
  • Accelerated timeline
  • Strong financials required
💰

Seller Financing

The seller carries a portion of the purchase price, reducing the senior lender's exposure and your cash requirement at close.

  • Negotiable terms
  • Signals seller confidence
  • Easier senior lender approval

What to prepare before applying.

Having these items ready accelerates the underwriting process substantially.

1

Target financials

Three years of tax returns, profit and loss statements, and balance sheets for the business you intend to acquire.

2

Personal financials

Your own tax returns, net worth statement, and credit history — required for any owner holding 20% or more.

3

Purchase agreement

A signed letter of intent or formal purchase agreement establishing the transaction's price and structure.
